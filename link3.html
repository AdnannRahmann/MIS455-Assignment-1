Morocco

Morocco, located in the northwest corner of Africa, is a country of diverse landscapes—deserts, mountains, forests, and coastlines. Here are some intriguing facts about this marvelous destination: First Recognition of America: In 1777, Morocco was the first country to recognize the United States as an independent nation. The Moroccan-American Friendship Treaty of 1876 remains the longest-standing agreement of its kind. Cell Phones Outnumber People: With a population of around 35 million, Morocco boasts more than 44 million active cell phones. It’s a tech-savvy country where mobile devices are the primary way to access the internet. Oldest Educational Institution: The University of Al Quaraouiyine, established in 859 in Fez, is the world’s oldest existing educational institution. Today, it focuses on Islamic legal and religious studies. Multilingual Society: Morocco has several official languages: Standard Arabic, Standard Berber, and French. Moroccan Arabic (Darija) is the native dialect, while Spanish is spoken in the northern region due to historic ties. Triple New Year Celebrations: Moroccans celebrate three New Year’s occasions: the Gregorian Calendar, the Amazigh New Year (Yennayer), and the Islamic New Year (Fatih Muharram). It’s a land of festivals and holidays. Dual Coastlines: Morocco’s stunning beaches stretch along both the Atlantic Ocean and the Mediterranean Sea. It’s one of only three countries (along with Spain and France) with both Atlantic and Mediterranean coastlines. From its rich history to its vibrant culture, Morocco continues to surprise and delight travelers

The Koutoubia Mosque, morocco

The Koutoubia Mosque, located in Marrakech, Morocco, is a remarkable architectural gem with a rich history. Here are some intriguing facts about this iconic mosque: Almohad Art and Grandeur: Built in the twelfth century, the Koutoubia Mosque exemplifies Almohad art. It is the largest mosque in Marrakech and stands as a testament to the city’s historical and cultural significance. Name Origins: The name “Koutoubia” means “the one of the booksellers” in Arabic. It refers to the presence of a bustling souk (market) of booksellers that developed around the mosque, with over a hundred stalls. Architectural Features: The mosque is constructed primarily of red stone, formerly plastered. Its 66-meter-high minaret dominates the skyline and is the tallest building in the city. The minaret includes gilded copper balls in the traditional Moroccan style. Legends of the Orbs: Legends surround the four orbs atop the minaret. One story claims they were originally made of pure gold and that the fourth was donated as penance for breaking the Ramadan fast. Another version suggests they were crafted from the jewels of a Sultan’s wife. Access and Symbolism: Access to the mosque’s interior is prohibited for non-Muslims (except for the Hassan II Mosque in Casablanca). The Koutoubia’s grandeur and symbolism make it a must-see for visitors to Marrakech. While you can’t enter the mosque, contemplating its exterior—just minutes from Jemaa el-Fna Square—is a sensory delight. If you have more time, explore the surrounding areas, such as Essaouira, the Ourika Valley, or Ouarzazate. The Koutoubia Mosque remains an enduring symbol of Marrakech’s heritage and spirituality.
