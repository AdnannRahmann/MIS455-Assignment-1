Kuwait

Kuwait, a Western Asian nation at the tip of the Persian Gulf, is associated with many fascinating facts. Let’s explore some of them: Expatriate Majority: Kuwaitis are a minority in their own country, as expatriates constitute 70% of the population. Most expats live in Kuwait City, where they work in various sectors of the economy. The largest expat community is Indian, with many hailing from the southern states of Tamil Nadu and Kerala. Egyptians form the second-largest expat group. Oil Wealth and Reserves: Kuwait holds 8% of the world’s oil reserves, making it the sixth-largest oil reserve globally. The Burgan field alone contains about 70 billion barrels of oil. High-Valued Currency: The Kuwaiti dinar is the currency, sub-divided into 1,000 fils. As of October 2019, it is the world’s highest-valued currency unit per face value. Introduced in 1960, it replaced the former currency, the Gulf rupee. Cultural Center and Opera House: The Sheikh Jaber Al-Ahmad Cultural Centre (JACC) in Kuwait City is the largest opera house in the Middle East. It hosts libraries, cinemas, exhibition halls, concert venues, and theaters, providing entertainment and education for Kuwaitis. Historical Origins: The name “Kuwait” is derived from an Arabic word meaning “fort.” Settlers fortified settlements along the coast of the Persian Gulf, leading to the name “Kuwait.” Rich Music Scene: Kuwait has a vibrant music scene, and several popular Arab musical genres were born here. One example is sawt, a complex form of urban music. In summary, Kuwait’s oil wealth, cultural diversity, and architectural landmarks make it a captivating country to explore

Kuwait Towers

The Kuwait Towers stand as iconic landmarks in Kuwait City, rising dramatically on a promontory into the Arabian Gulf. Designed by Danish architect Malene Bjørn, they were officially inaugurated in March 1979 and are regarded as symbols of modern Kuwait. Here’s what makes them fascinating: Distinctive Architecture: The main tower, soaring 187 meters (614 feet) high, carries two spheres. The lower sphere houses a massive water tank (4,500 cubic meters) and an upper-level restaurant with panoramic views. The upper sphere, rotating every 30 minutes, hosts a café. A second tower serves as a water reservoir, while the third houses equipment to illuminate the larger towers. Enamel Steel Discs: Approximately 41,000 enameled steel discs cover the three spheres in shades of blue, green, and gray. These discs evoke the intricate tiled domes of historic mosques, adding an artistic touch to the towers. Historical Origins: The Kuwait Towers were part of a water distribution project by the Swedish engineering company VBB. The Emir of Kuwait, Sheikh Jaber Al-Ahmed, wanted a more attractive design for the sixth site. Out of ten different designs, the chosen one became the striking Kuwait Towers. Cultural Significance: The towers represent not only water storage but also Kuwait’s resilience and progress. They have witnessed the nation’s growth, from pearl diving to oil wealth, and remain a testament to its heritage. Night Illumination: At night, the towers come alive with vibrant lighting, casting reflections on the Gulf waters. They serve as beacons, guiding ships and symbolizing Kuwait’s connection to the sea. In summary, the Kuwait Towers blend functionality, aesthetics, and cultural pride, standing as enduring symbols of this dynamic nation.
